About the Project

Where Biology
Meets Photonics

The Blue Morpho butterfly (Morpho menelaus) produces its stunning iridescent blue not from pigment — but from physics. Microscopic ridged lamellae on each wing scale act as a biological diffraction grating, producing vivid colour through thin-film interference.

This simulator models that phenomenon in real time, letting you manipulate wavelength, angle of incidence, film thickness, and camera perspective to observe how structural colour emerges from quantum-scale geometry.

The Optics

Science Behind the Shimmer

01
⚡

Thin-Film Interference

Light reflecting from multiple chitin-air interfaces creates path length differences. When ΔL = mλ, constructive interference amplifies that wavelength to brilliant intensity.

2nd cos θ = mλ
02
🔷

Nanostructure Geometry

Each wing scale carries 200nm-thick lamellae spaced ~230nm apart. This biological photonic crystal selectively reflects blue light while transmitting others.

~200nm film
03
📐

Angle Dependence

Increasing the angle of incidence θ shortens the effective optical path, shifting the peak wavelength toward blue (blueshifting). This is why the wings flash different colours as you tilt them.

n=1.56 chitin
04
🌊

Coherent Scattering

Unlike incoherent scattering (Rayleigh/Mie), the wing's periodic structure creates long-range order that produces coherent constructive interference — making the colour extraordinarily saturated.

photonic crystal
05
🦠

Evolutionary Optics

The nanostructure evolved independently in multiple Lepidoptera lineages — a remarkable case of convergent evolution driven by mate selection and predator avoidance.

convergent evolution
06
💡

Biomimetic Applications

Morpho-inspired photonics drives angle-independent displays, anti-counterfeiting holographics, radiative cooling textiles, and self-cleaning hydrophobic surfaces.

biomimicry

Photonic crystal arrays
